Overview

Swiggy is built for someone who can read, tap, and type with confidence.

That quiet assumption locks out one of India's fastest-growing user groups — older adults who would rather speak their order than fight an unfamiliar screen. I led the discovery and product definition for Voice to Order, a feature that lets anyone place a complete order just by speaking, in their own language.

Elderly users can't navigate the app independently
The solution

Context — this case study was produced as a capstone during an intensive product management programme. The research framing and product decisions are my own; the metrics below are pre-launch targets I defined as part of the product definition, not measured results.
The problem

Most elderly users can't place an order on their own. So they don't.

When a senior citizen wants food delivered, they rarely open the app themselves. They call a son or daughter, often living in another city, and ask them to order on their behalf. A two-minute task becomes a back-and-forth across a family — repeated calls, miscommunication, wrong items, missed deliveries.

The person who actually needs the food has no control over getting it. When family isn't available, they simply go without.

The core insight
Elderly users aren't bad with technology — many use YouTube and WhatsApp comfortably. The real issue is that Swiggy's app assumes every user can read English, recognise icons, and confidently tap through several screens. That assumption is what locks them out — not their age.

Why the app fails them — six recurring barriers, each compounding the next.

Low learnability
New interfaces take too long to grasp
Poor readability
Small text, low contrast, deep menus
Tiny touch targets
Buttons too small for imprecise hands
Fear of errors
One error message ends the whole task
Cognitive overload
Too many actions crammed in one flow
Unfamiliar patterns
Swipes, gestures and jargon feel alien

India's elderly population is large and growing fast — and existing research on elderly digital behaviour suggests a meaningful share struggle to complete tasks like food ordering on apps built for younger, English-fluent users. I used this directional insight from population data and prior studies to size the opportunity and justify why this was worth solving — not as a number I personally measured, but as the reasoning behind prioritising the problem.

Who I designed for

One person. One barrier. The hardest case I could find.

I deliberately designed for the user with the least ability to use the existing app — not the average user. If the solution works for her, it works for everyone above her on the ability curve.

Designing for the extreme made the product stronger for everyone else, not weaker for her.

Research and insights

What actually justified building voice, not just a simpler screen.

Before designing anything, I needed evidence that voice — not bigger buttons or fewer steps — was the right answer. Each insight below is one piece of that evidence, showing why a familiar, error-safe, cost-transparent voice experience matched how this user actually behaves.

01
Confidence in repetition
Comfortable with familiar actions — avoids exploring new ones, so the solution needed to feel instantly familiar, not novel.
02
Tap, not swipe
Knows how to tap to open something; gestures like swiping are foreign — confirming screen navigation itself is a barrier.
03
Voice over typing
Many prefer speaking into a mic over typing — this single pattern is the actual reason voice was the right solution, not a UI tweak.
04
Fear of errors
An error screen causes real anxiety and total task abandonment — this is why a confirm-before-placing safety screen was non-negotiable.
05
Cautious about cost
Highly aware of pricing — this is why the order total is read back clearly before any payment is confirmed.
The decision this unlocked

Insight 03 was the hinge. It meant the answer wasn't "a simpler interface" — it was removing the interface from the critical path entirely. Voice wasn't a nice-to-have here. It was the most accessible option available, and the timing lined up too: a massive underserved market, no competitor offering voice ordering yet, and Indic-language AI models that had just become mature enough to make this technically viable.

The solution

Speak naturally. The AI handles everything else.

Voice to Order is a fully automated AI voice agent built into the Swiggy app. The user taps one button, speaks in whatever language is comfortable, and the AI handles understanding, confirming, and placing the order — start to finish, with no typing or screen navigation.

The one principle behind every decision
Language is never something the user has to choose. The AI detects it automatically from the first spoken word. Asking a 65-year-old to pick a language from a list first would reintroduce the exact barrier this feature exists to remove.

Three conversation flows — because people don't all think about ordering the same way.

Key decisions

Defining this feature meant choosing what not to build.

Decided to include
✓Auto language detection from speech, zero setup required
✓Full voice confirmation before any order is placed
✓Cash on delivery as default for new and assisted users
✓On-screen confirmation as a visual safety net
✓"Pay later" path for users who need family to pay
Deliberately deferred
—A dedicated phone line, kept in-app to reuse existing login
—Human agent fallback, measure failure rate before adding one
—Language selection menus, replaced entirely by auto-detect
—Support beyond 6 languages, quality bar over breadth first
—Voice payments, too risky for v1 trust, confirm visually instead
The trade-off I'm proudest of

Going fully automated with no human fallback was the riskiest call I made. A human handoff would have capped the feature's scale and economics from day one. Instead I set a hard guardrail — if AI misunderstanding exceeds 10%, it triggers a quality review — so the team would learn fast without quietly shipping a broken experience to the people who could least afford a bad one.

Success metrics

How I'd know if it actually worked.

A new feature's targets are hypotheses, not guarantees. I set them as thresholds that tell the team whether to keep investing, fix, or stop — each tied to a reason, not pulled from the air.

North star metric
Call-to-order conversion rate ≥ 65%
This is the one number that tells you the entire voice experience is working end to end — not just that people are trying it, but that it is successfully resolving into a completed order. Every other metric below explains movement in this number.
Metric
Target
Why this number
Monthly active voice users
5,000
A conservative, credible beta target against Swiggy's 100M+ base — roughly 0.005% adoption.
Avg. order call duration
≤ 3 min
Standard app ordering takes 2–4 min. If voice is slower, it has no reason to exist.
AI misunderstanding rate
< 10%
More than 1 in 10 errors permanently erodes trust, especially for first-time elderly users.
Post-call satisfaction
≥ 4.0/5
Industry-standard threshold for a "good" experience; below it triggers a review.
On honesty — these are pre-launch targets, not measured results. The right answer to "where's the data?" is that these are thresholds set before launch, to be replaced by real analytics after a discovery sprint with the data team. A new feature has hypotheses, not history.
Go-to-market

A quality-gated, staged rollout.

Rather than launching everywhere at once, I planned an 18-week phased roadmap that proves one language works perfectly before scaling — because a bad first experience with voice, for this audience, means they never come back.

1
Weeks 1–6 · Foundation — Tamil-first, single city
Nail one language end to end. Internal testing with real elderly users in Chennai, establishing the AI quality baseline before adding any breadth.
2
Weeks 7–14 · Closed beta — Chennai, guardrails live
Limited rollout with every guardrail metric instrumented. Watching misunderstanding rate and drop-off closely before adding a second language.
3
Weeks 15–18 · Scale — more languages, more cities
Remaining languages and Tier 2/3 cities roll out, gated on each previous language hitting its CSAT and conversion targets. Quality bar before speed bar, always.
Reflection

What I'd do differently.

The most useful part of any project is what it teaches you. A few honest takeaways from owning this end to end.

01
Validate the research before building
My problem framing leaned on a directional stat. With more time, I'd run a proper survey to firm up the non-ordering rate among older users before committing engineering scope.
02
Test the no-fallback bet earlier
Full automation is a bold call. I'd pressure-test it with a small Wizard-of-Oz prototype, a human simulating the AI, to measure real misunderstanding rates before betting the architecture on it.
03
Design the failure states deeper
I covered the happy path and the unavailable-item case well, but production needs richer handling for accents, background noise, and mid-order changes — that's where voice products live or die.
04
Bring in unit economics sooner
Voice AI cost per call directly shapes whether full automation is viable at scale. I'd model cost-per-order against COD margins earlier to stress-test the business case.
The biggest lesson
Designing for the user with the least ability, not the average user, produced a sharper product for everyone. Accessibility wasn't a constraint on this feature. It was the entire reason it was worth building.
